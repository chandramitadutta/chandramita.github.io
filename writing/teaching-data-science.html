What Two Years of Teaching Data Science Taught Me About Learning

Reflections from two years as a Graduate Student Instructor at UC Berkeley — on questions, confusion, and what it actually means to understand something.

The students who struggled most with the math were often the ones asking the most interesting questions. Not "how do I compute this?" but "why does the model think this?" and "what does it mean that the loss went down?" — questions that cut to the actual heart of what we were doing, questions I had to sit with before I could answer.

I spent two years as a Graduate Student Instructor in UC Berkeley's data science program. I came in thinking I would be teaching students. I left having learned at least as much as I gave.

The questions that matter aren't the ones you expect

In my first semester, I prepared obsessively. I knew the material. I could derive the math, run the code, explain the theory. What I wasn't prepared for was the student who, midway through a lecture on gradient descent, raised their hand and asked: "But how does the model know it's wrong?"

It took me a moment. The technical answer is straightforward — the loss function quantifies error, backpropagation distributes the blame. But the question wasn't really about the math. It was about epistemology. How does any learner — human or machine — know when they're wrong?

"The question isn't whether you got the right answer. The question is whether you understand why the wrong answer was wrong."

I started noticing this pattern. The students who seemed to be struggling, who got lost in the notation and stumbled on the syntax, were often the ones who were most genuinely engaged with what the model was doing. The students who breezed through the problem sets sometimes struggled most when the problem changed slightly — because they had learned the procedure, not the principle.

Confusion is a compass, not a verdict

What I kept coming back to was the idea that confusion, when it's the right kind of confusion, is a signal that you're near something true and difficult — not that you're lost. The students who came to office hours saying "I don't understand this at all" were often closer to understanding than the ones who said "I think I got it."

What teaching gave me

I became a better data scientist in those two years. Not because I reviewed the fundamentals — though that helped — but because explaining something to someone who doesn't know it yet forces you to understand it differently. You stop being able to hide behind the jargon. You have to find the actual shape of the idea.

I also became more patient with my own learning. Watching a room full of people wrestle with a concept I'd once wrestled with myself, I stopped expecting understanding to arrive quickly or completely. It comes in pieces, and the pieces don't always arrive in the right order.
